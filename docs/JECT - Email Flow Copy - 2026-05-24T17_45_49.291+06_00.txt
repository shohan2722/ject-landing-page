The Core Treatment Categories — and What They're Actually For
WRINKLE REDUCERS (Botox and Similar Neuromodulators)


What they address: Dynamic wrinkles — lines that form as a result of repeated muscle movement. Forehead lines, crow's feet, the "11s" between your brows, bunny lines on the nose, lip lines, neck bands.


How they work: Temporarily relax the muscle responsible for the movement that creates the line. The line softens or disappears — and, over time, consistent treatment can actually prevent deeper lines from forming.


What they don't address: Lines that are present even when your face is completely still (these are called static wrinkles and often need filler or skin resurfacing). Volume loss. Skin texture or pigmentation.


How long results last: Typically 3–4 months before a maintenance treatment is beneficial.


Who benefits most: People noticing lines related to expression — especially in the forehead, around the eyes, and between the brows. Also highly effective as a preventative treatment before lines become deeply etched.




DERMAL FILLERS


What they address: Volume loss, static lines, contour definition, and areas where structural support has diminished over time. Common areas include cheeks, lips, nasolabial folds, under-eye hollowness, jawline, and chin.


How they work: Hyaluronic acid-based fillers restore volume and structure to areas where natural volume has been lost — or where strategic addition creates a more balanced, harmonious facial appearance.


What they don't address: Dynamic wrinkles caused by muscle movement (this is where wrinkle reducers come in). Skin texture, pigmentation, or collagen quality.


How long results last: Varies by product and area — typically 6–18 months.


Who benefits most: People noticing facial hollowness, volume deflation in cheeks or under-eyes, thinning lips, or a loss of definition along the jaw or chin. Also effective for people who look "tired" even when fully rested, as this often reflects volume loss rather than expression lines.




LASER AND LIGHT TREATMENTS (MOXI, BBL, and Others)


What they address: Skin quality issues that live at the surface and just beneath it. Sun damage, hyperpigmentation, age spots, redness, uneven tone, overall skin dullness, early texture irregularities, and mild laxity.


How they work: MOXI delivers fractional laser energy to stimulate collagen and improve tone and texture. BBL (BroadBand Light) uses light energy to target pigmentation, redness, and vascular irregularities — and is one of the few treatments with evidence suggesting it can actually slow the visible signs of skin aging at a cellular level.


What they don't address: Volume loss (that's filler territory). Deep static wrinkles. Active acne (there are specific protocols for this).


Downtime: MOXI — typically 4–5 days of visible skin "shedding" as treated areas clear. BBL — minimal, often 24–48 hours of mild redness.


How long results last: Collagen-stimulating effects continue developing for 3–6 months. A single treatment can produce visible improvement for 12+ months; many clients do annual or twice-annual maintenance treatments.


Who benefits most: People dealing with sun damage, uneven skin tone, age spots, redness, or dullness who want visible skin quality improvement rather than (or in addition to) volume or line correction.




MICRONEEDLING


What they address: Skin texture, pore size, acne scarring, fine lines, and collagen quality. One of the most effective treatments for improving overall skin structure rather than surface-level concerns.


How it works: Creates controlled micro-injuries that trigger the skin's natural healing response — collagen and elastin production increases, texture smooths, scars fade, and skin quality improves from within.


What they don't address: Volume loss. Pigmentation (though some microneedling protocols include targeted serums that address this in combination). Deep dynamic wrinkles.


Downtime: 24–48 hours of redness, similar to a mild sunburn. Full results develop over 4–8 weeks as collagen production peaks.


How long results last: Results from a full series (typically 3–4 sessions) can last 12 months or longer. Maintenance sessions keep collagen production active.


Who benefits most: People dealing with acne scarring, enlarged pores, skin texture irregularities, or early signs of collagen loss. Also very effective as a "foundation" treatment before adding other modalities.




AQUAGOLD FINE TOUCH


What it addresses: Skin radiance, fine surface lines, pore appearance, and overall glow. This is the treatment that produces the "my skin just looks good" reaction rather than any single targeted correction.


How it works: A micro-infusion device delivers a customized cocktail of hyaluronic acid, vitamins, and skin-enhancing actives directly into the superficial skin — essentially flooding the skin with everything it needs to look luminous.


What it doesn't address: Deeper structural concerns, volume loss, or significant pigmentation.


Downtime: Minimal — most clients are social-event-ready within 24–48 hours. Glow appears within days and peaks around 2–4 weeks.


Who benefits most: People preparing for a significant event (weddings, shoots, milestone occasions) who want their skin to look effortlessly luminous. Also effective as a monthly or quarterly maintenance treatment for clients who prioritize skin radiance.




CHEMICAL PEELS


What they address: Surface-level texture, dullness, mild hyperpigmentation, congestion, and overall skin renewal. The JECT menu includes light, medium, and deeper peel options depending on your concern level and downtime tolerance.


How they work: Controlled chemical exfoliation removes damaged surface cells, accelerates cell turnover, and stimulates new skin growth beneath.


Downtime: Ranges from zero (light peels) to 5–7 days of visible peeling (deeper clinical peels).


Who benefits most: People dealing with dullness, mild discoloration, congestion, or acne-related texture — especially as a regular maintenance treatment between more intensive sessions.

The Preventative Advantage — And Why It's More Significant Than Marketing Language Suggests
If you've been around the aesthetics space at all, you've heard the term "preventative Botox." It's been covered extensively — Vogue, the New York Times, Reddit threads, dermatology forums.


But most of the coverage treats it as a trend. We want to explain the actual mechanism, because it's not a trend. It's physiology.


Here's what's actually happening:


Lines form through a combination of two processes. The first is intrinsic aging — natural collagen loss, skin thinning, and structural changes that occur over time. The second is dynamic muscle activity — the repeated folding and creasing of skin over the same pathways, day after day, year after year.


When you raise your eyebrows, furrow your brow, squint, or smile — you're creating a crease in the same location, over and over. Eventually that crease deepens from a temporary expression line into a static line that's present even when your face is at rest.


Wrinkle reducers work by temporarily interrupting the muscle activity that creates those lines. When you start treatment before a line has fully formed or deepened, you're essentially preventing the crease from reaching that depth. The skin rests. The collagen in that area isn't being repeatedly stressed.


The result: People who start preventative treatment earlier — typically in their mid-to-late twenties or early thirties — often require less product over time, achieve more subtle results more consistently, and age more gradually than people who start later because they're addressing existing lines rather than preventing them.


This isn't us encouraging you to get Botox at 22. It's an honest explanation of why your provider will likely discuss your current skin trajectory and your goals over the next 5–10 years at your consultation — not just what's bothering you today.




Treatment Timelines: What Most People Don't Know About Event Prep
If you have something on the calendar — a wedding, a reunion, a significant photographic event — the timing of your treatments matters in ways that aren't always obvious.


Here's the general framework your provider will walk you through (this is not a prescription — every client's timeline is personalized, but this gives you a realistic picture):


Wrinkle reducers: Should ideally be performed 2–4 weeks before an event. This allows time for the product to fully settle and for any mild initial effects (minor heaviness, rare initial bruising) to fully resolve. First-time treatment? Allow the full 4 weeks to understand how your specific muscles respond before your event.


Dermal fillers: Ideally 4–6 weeks before an event. Swelling from filler injections can take 1–2 weeks to fully resolve, and results look their best once everything has settled. First-time filler in a specific area? Allow 6+ weeks.


Laser treatments (MOXI, BBL): 4–6 weeks before an event is typical, as these treatments involve a recovery period (MOXI in particular involves several days of visible skin shedding as the treated cells come to the surface). The final result — the glowing, improved skin quality you're after — develops over 4–8 weeks as collagen responds.


Microneedling: A full series of 3–4 treatments produces the best results, and results continue developing over 4–8 weeks after each session. For a wedding, a microneedling series ideally begins 6 months before the event, with a final light touch-up at the appropriate interval before the date.


Aquagold: This is the exception — a treatment specifically designed for a pre-event glow that can be performed 2–3 weeks before an event. Immediate hydration and radiance, minimal downtime.


The important implication: If you're waiting until 3 weeks before your event to figure this out, your options are significantly more limited than if you start planning now.

The Financial Timing Question — The Cost of Waiting
We're going to say something that might sound counterintuitive from a business that wants to book appointments:


Waiting isn't always wrong. But waiting while your concerns compound has a real cost — and it's often more than the cost of starting earlier would have been.


Here's what we mean:


A dynamic wrinkle that's just beginning to form — a light expression line, a subtle crease — typically responds to a conservative dose of wrinkle reducer. It settles cleanly. The result looks effortlessly natural.


The same line, three years later, has become a static wrinkle — present even when the face is completely still, with some degree of skin folding that has developed over time. Treating it now still works. But it may require a combination of approaches: wrinkle reducer plus potentially some filler to address the static component. The investment is higher. The recovery expectation is longer. The result, while still achievable, requires more.


Preventative treatment isn't just philosophically better. It's often practically more economical over a 5–10 year horizon.

* Text-to-Image Ratio: Maintain minimum 60% text, maximum 40% images in email body

* Spam Trigger Words: Avoid "free," "guarantee," "100%," "limited time offer" in subject lines — use benefit language instead

* Double Opt-In Option: Consider for quality improvement; reduces list size but improves engagement rates and deliverability

SECTION 10: MOBILE OPTIMIZATION
Complete Mobile Optimization Strategy


Subject Line Length Optimization for Mobile Preview
Mobile email clients (Gmail on iOS, Apple Mail) typically display 30–40 characters in the subject line preview on most devices, with preview text showing approximately 85–100 characters total (subject + preview combined).


Optimized subject line structure:






Email Formatting for Mobile Readability
Paragraph length: Maximum 3 lines at mobile viewport (375px) before a visual break. Long unbroken paragraphs are the fastest way to lose mobile readers — even when the content is valuable.


Font size: Minimum 16px for body text; 18–20px recommended. 14px and below is consistently cited as the leading cause of mobile email abandonment.


Line height: 1.5–1.6x font size for optimal readability; avoid single-spaced body text.


Email width: 600px maximum for desktop; scales fluidly to device width on mobile. Single-column layout throughout — two-column breaks down unpredictably on mobile.


Image specifications:
* All images must include descriptive alt text (displays if images are blocked)
* Max width: 600px at 2x resolution for retina displays (1200px actual file)
* Format: WebP with JPEG fallback
* File size: Under 150kb per image
* Emails should be fully readable with images disabled (do not embed critical information in images)




CTA Placement and Sizing for Touch Interfaces
Minimum tap target size: 44x44 points (Apple HIG) / 48x48dp (Material Design)


Recommended CTA button specifications:
* Height: Minimum 52px
* Width: Full-width on mobile (max 340px)
* Padding: 16px top/bottom, 24px left/right
* Font: 16–18px, bold weight
* Color: High contrast against background (JECT brand palette — validate contrast ratio against WCAG AA standard)
* Placement: Below a clear value statement, not floating without context


CTA spacing: Minimum 16px between CTA button and surrounding text. On mobile, fingers have less precision than mouse cursors — sufficient spacing prevents accidental adjacent clicks.


Multiple CTAs: When offering more than one CTA in an email, stack vertically on mobile with clear visual hierarchy. Primary CTA: full-width, high contrast. Secondary CTA: text hyperlink or outlined button below primary.




Mobile-Specific A/B Tests




Load Time Optimization for Mobile Networks
* Total email file size: Target under 100kb for the full HTML email (excluding linked images)
* Hosted images: All images hosted on CDN (not embedded/inline); reduces email file size dramatically
* Font loading: Use web-safe fallback fonts rather than Google Fonts where possible; custom fonts add load time
* CSS: Inline CSS only (email clients strip <head> CSS); keep declarations minimal
* Video: Never embed video directly in email (not supported across clients); use video thumbnail with linked play button instead
* GIFs: If using animated GIFs, keep under 500kb and ensure the first frame communicates the message (many clients show only first frame)
